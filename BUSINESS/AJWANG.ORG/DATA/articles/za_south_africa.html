Trade & Investment Climate

South Africa is a signatory to 6 international investment agreements, including bilateral investment treaties (BITs) and free trade agreements. These treaties provide legal protections for foreign investors, covering fair treatment, expropriation compensation, and dispute resolution mechanisms.

The country's Corruption Perceptions Index (CPI) score is 41/100 (Moderate) (Transparency International, 2025). Investors should account for governance considerations when structuring transactions and contracts.

Doing Business: Practical Steps

  1. Choose your entry structure: Foreign investors can enter South Africa through a wholly-owned subsidiary, joint venture with a local partner, branch office, or representative office. Each structure has different regulatory, tax, and liability implications.
  2. Register your company: Business registration is handled by the national companies registry. Typical requirements include memorandum and articles of association, director/shareholder details, registered office address, and initial capital deposit. Costs and timelines vary — budget 2–8 weeks and legal fees.
  3. Open a business bank account: Major international banks operating in South Africa include local branches of Standard Chartered, Ecobank, Stanbic, and regional development banks. Foreign companies will need notarised incorporation documents and proof of business activity.
  4. Understand taxation: Research current corporate income tax rates, VAT registration thresholds, withholding tax on dividends and royalties, and any sector-specific levies. Tax treaties with your home country may reduce withholding rates.
  5. Hire and comply: Local labour laws govern minimum wages, contracts, social security contributions, and termination procedures. Foreign workers typically require work permits sponsored by the registered entity.

Key Sectors for Investment

International investors in South Africa find opportunities across multiple sectors. Agriculture and agro-processing, construction and infrastructure, hospitality and tourism, manufacturing, financial services, and technology represent consistently active areas. Sector-specific licensing and local ownership requirements may apply — legal due diligence is essential before committing capital.
